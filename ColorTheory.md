# Color Theory

A summary of color theory fundamentals, based on [Figma's color theory guide](https://www.figma.com/resource-library/what-is-color-theory/).

## What Is Color Theory?

Color theory helps artists, designers, and product teams understand how colors work together to achieve color harmony. It provides a framework for choosing and combining colors effectively using the color wheel and established harmonies.

## The Color Wheel

The color wheel organizes colors based on their relationships and is the foundation of color theory.

### Color Types

- **Primary colors** — Red, yellow, and blue (RYB). These cannot be created by mixing other colors and serve as the base for all other colors.
- **Secondary colors** — Orange, green, and violet. Created by mixing two primary colors (e.g., red + yellow = orange).
- **Tertiary colors** — Red-orange, yellow-orange, yellow-green, blue-green, blue-violet, and red-violet. Created by mixing a primary and an adjacent secondary color.

### Warm vs. Cool Colors

- **Warm colors** (red, orange, yellow) — Convey energy, intensity, and excitement.
- **Cool colors** (blue, green, violet) — Evoke calmness, relaxation, and trust.

## Color Properties

Every color has three adjustable properties:

| Property       | Description                                                    |
|----------------|----------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Hue**        | The color itself (e.g., red, blue, green)                      |
| **Saturation** | Color intensity — from vivid (100%) to muted/gray (0%)         |
| **Value**      | Lightness or darkness — from bright (100%) to black (0%)       |

### Tints, Shades, and Tones

- **Tint** — A color mixed with white (lighter, softer)
- **Shade** — A color mixed with black (darker, deeper)
- **Tone** — A color mixed with gray (more muted, subtle)

These variations are the basis for monochromatic color schemes and are useful for creating depth and hierarchy in UI.

## Color Harmonies

Color harmonies are combinations of colors that produce a visually pleasing effect. There are six main types:

### 1. Complementary

Two colors opposite each other on the color wheel (e.g., blue and orange). Provides the strongest contrast of any scheme — great for drawing attention to key elements.

### 2. Analogous

Three colors adjacent on the color wheel (e.g., red, orange, yellow). Creates a harmonious, cohesive feel. Easy to work with and looks richer than monochromatic.

### 3. Triadic

Three colors equally spaced on the color wheel (e.g., red, yellow, blue). Offers strong visual contrast while retaining balance and color richness.

### 4. Split-Complementary

A base color plus the two colors adjacent to its complement (e.g., green, red-orange, red-violet). High contrast without the strong tension of a full complementary scheme.

### 5. Tetradic (Square/Rectangle)

Four colors evenly spaced on the color wheel. Works best when one color dominates and the others serve as accents. More complex and harder to balance, but creates vivid contrast.

### 6. Monochromatic

Variations of a single hue using different tints, shades, and tones (e.g., light blue, medium blue, dark blue). Clean, elegant, and soothing — excellent for creating a unified look.

## Applying Color Theory to Mobile App Design

When choosing a color palette for an app:

1. **Start with brand identity** — Pick a primary color that reflects the brand's personality and values.
2. **Build a hierarchy** — Use a dominant color for primary actions, secondary colors for supporting elements, and neutral colors for backgrounds and text.
3. **Leverage contrast** — Ensure sufficient contrast between text and background for readability and accessibility.
4. **Limit the palette** — Stick to 2–3 main colors plus neutrals to avoid visual clutter.
5. **Test in context** — Colors behave differently on screens vs. swatches; always validate in real UI layouts.
